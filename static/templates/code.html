tallier

While working at reddit in 2012, I set up an instance of etsy's statsd service, to collect live stats from all our app servers. Unfortunately, as a node.js service, statsd couldn't make good use of our multi-core CPU. We quickly topped out the amount of data we could collect.

To cope with this, I wrote a new implementation in Python, so I could spread the work across the entire CPU (with the multiprocessing module). We were able to collect roughly three times as much data as before. I called this rewrite "tallier" (because it buffers up tallies of thousands of keys before flushing them to graphite). This served us well for about a year, until once again we started running into the limit of our processing power. Rather than spend more money for beefier EC2 instances, I decided to try writing it in Go.

This was a fun and appropriate exercise for learning Go. It didn't take long before I had a complete replacement that was multiple times faster than the Python implementation. With some optimizations (and, eventually, the release of Go 1.2), we were able to collect all of the data we wanted with plenty of capacity to spare.

ibis

source: github.com/logan/ibis

In early 2014 I wanted to explore building highly scalable web applications with Go and Cassandra. To help manage mapping Go data structures to column families, I developed ibis.

This is very much a work in progress, but at the moment ibis provides some higher-level helper routines for working with gocql, as well as some reflection automagic to reduce boilerplate.

ircquotes

site: iq.appspot.com
source: github.com/logan/iq

In 2002 my friends and I were unsatisfied with the state of the art in posting funny quotes from IRC. Existing sites had unengaged moderators as gatekeepers, who didn't seem to have much effect on quality. I decided to make my own, replacing moderators and ranking with a pretty effective recommender system.

That original implementation is lost in the mists of time, but in 2008 I reimplemented it on Google App Engine. All the data has survived, but I never migrated the recommender over.
